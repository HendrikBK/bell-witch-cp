John Johnson, perhaps, took more interest in
the investigations than any other man; in fact,
from all I could gather. he was the leader and
inventor of most all the schemes resorted to. He
was a man of splendid endowments, keen obser-
vation, quick perception, and close comprehen-
sion ; self-willed and self-possessed, sustaining an
unsullied and intrepid character, Moreover, he
was less given than most of men to the supersti-
tious ideas that characterized the people in that
age, and as he told me, he entered into the inves-
tigation believing that some human agency was
at the bottom of the strange manifestations, and
he was determined to find it out if possible.

Knowing Mr. Johnson and others who lived
